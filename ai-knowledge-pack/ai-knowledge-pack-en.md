## FILE: CORE_QUESTION_EN.txt

Core question in plain language:
The central issue addressed here is whether a responsible human can still stop, pause, re-check, or redirect an AI-mediated decision, action, deployment, transaction, access change, external release, or production change before it reaches an irreversible outcome.

Why this matters:
Many governance systems ask whether human oversight exists. This question is narrower. It asks whether that oversight is still effective at the point when a decision or action would otherwise become difficult or impossible to undo.

Typical irreversible outcomes include:
- production deployment
- external data release
- contract or purchase finalization
- credential or permission changes
- account recovery or identity actions
- deletion or loss of recovery options
- irreversible rollback suppression
- tool execution with external or operational effects

The practical value of the question is that it can be added to an existing AI review, procurement review, security review, audit note, or change-management process without replacing that process.

## FILE: STATUS_AND_SCOPE_EN.txt

Current status:
LUMINA-30 is an independent, non-binding public reference framework produced by an individual or small project. At present, this pack is not an official standard, certification scheme, legal requirement, safety guarantee, peer-reviewed control framework, or evidence of third-party adoption, endorsement, or institutional verification.

What this status means:
The lack of official status means that organizations should not treat LUMINA-30 as authority, certification, compliance proof, or safety validation. It may still be reviewed as a low-cost way to make one prior condition explicit inside existing processes: whether responsible humans can still intervene before irreversible outcomes.

Scope:
LUMINA-30 does not replace law, audit, engineering safety, cybersecurity controls, model governance, procurement due diligence, organizational authority, incident response, or human judgment. It addresses a boundary question that can be inserted into those processes.

Relation to existing frameworks:
If an existing framework already implements the same check with clear timing, responsible human authority, evidence, rollback or suspension path, independent refusal channel, and No / Unknown handling, the LUMINA-30 name does not need to be adopted. Functional absorption of the condition is sufficient.

External references:
External AI commentary, company names, research project names, current events, search visibility, or prior AI evaluations are not evidence of adoption, endorsement, validation, or implementation.

## FILE: PRACTICAL_ENTRY_EN.txt

Practical first use:
Use the One-Question Pilot as the first operational entry point. Add one question to the existing review:

> Before this AI-mediated process reaches an irreversible outcome, can a responsible human still stop, pause, re-check, or redirect it?

Suggested answers:
- Yes: record the authority, evidence, timing, and intervention channel.
- No: do not treat human oversight as effective for this boundary; escalate or redesign.
- Unknown: hold the boundary claim as unverified; escalate for clarification before irreversible action.

Minimum record fields:
- Irreversible point: what becomes difficult or impossible to undo?
- Responsible authority: who can stop, pause, re-check, or redirect?
- Intervention timing: when can they still intervene?
- Authority basis: what gives them that authority?
- Evidence: what shows the intervention is still effective?
- Independent refusal channel: can the refusal or stopping path operate without relying on the evaluated AI system?
- Time margin: is there enough time between detection and irreversibility?
- No / Unknown handling: who receives escalation, and what action follows?

Natural insertion points:
- NIST AI RMF: human-AI configuration, monitoring, escalation, incident response, rollback, and risk management records.
- EU AI Act: human oversight assessment, especially where the existence of an override or intervention feature must be checked for timing and effectiveness.
- ISO/IEC 42001: impact assessment, operational planning, change management, deployment review, and monitoring controls.
- Internal AI review forms: place the question immediately before deployment approval, vendor approval, production change, external release, or credential-impacting action.

## FILE: TERMINOLOGY_EN.txt

Purpose of terminology:
The following terms are labels used in LUMINA-30 materials for cross-reference. A reader can understand the core issue without accepting or memorizing these labels.

LUMINA-30:
The name of the non-binding public reference framework that centers the pre-irreversibility human intervention question.

Effective human refusal:
The practical ability of a responsible human or legitimate oversight body to stop, pause, withhold, challenge, re-check, redirect, suspend, or roll back an AI-mediated process before irreversible consequences occur.

Irreversibility:
The point at which an outcome, commitment, exposure, deployment, access change, external effect, or loss of recovery option can no longer be practically prevented, reversed, or meaningfully reviewed in time.

PCR-C:
The internal shorthand used in LUMINA-30 materials for the core condition: effective human refusal before irreversibility. It is not an official standard, legal requirement, or empirically validated control name.

Boundary Kernel:
A term used in earlier LUMINA-30 materials for a reference block that preserved the core condition during AI-assisted summaries. In this V8 reader-facing pack, the practically useful content is presented instead as the checklist in `FILE: OVERSIGHT_FAILURE_PATTERNS_EN.txt`.

Human Anchor:
A background label used in some LUMINA-30 materials for the role of accountable human authority. It is not necessary for first use of the One-Question Pilot.

Reversible Prosperity Path:
A positive framing for the direction opened when irreversible escalation is avoided and effective human refusal remains possible. It is not an alternative name for LUMINA-30.

## FILE: OVERSIGHT_FAILURE_PATTERNS_EN.txt

Apparent oversight that may fail in practice:
The following patterns help reviewers distinguish formal oversight from effective human refusal before irreversibility.

1. Formal approver only
A name appears in an approval field, but that person lacks the time, information, authority, or independent channel needed to intervene before irreversible action.

2. Logs without usable intervention
Logs exist, but they are only available after irreversible deployment, release, transaction, deletion, access change, or loss of rollback.

3. Procedural appearance
The workflow contains review steps, tickets, sign-offs, or dashboards, but the operational path continues automatically before a human can meaningfully stop or redirect it.

4. AI-generated assurance
The evaluated system, or a dependent AI process, claims that oversight is sufficient. That assurance is not a substitute for independently verifiable human authority and intervention evidence.

5. Dependent refusal channel
The path for refusal, stopping, appeal, escalation, or rollback depends on the same AI system or automation being evaluated. This weakens independence.

6. No time margin
The time between detection and irreversible action is too short for a responsible human to understand the situation and intervene.

7. Authority without evidence
A human nominally has authority, but the record does not show what evidence they can see before irreversibility.

8. Rollback loss
The process allows approval or deployment but does not preserve a practical pause, rollback, suspension, or recovery path before the irreversible point.

Use of this appendix:
These patterns are not a certification checklist. They are prompts for asking whether the organization’s existing oversight is still effective before irreversible outcomes.
